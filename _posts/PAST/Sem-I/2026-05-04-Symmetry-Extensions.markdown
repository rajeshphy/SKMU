In the previous notes, a canonical transformation was defined by the condition that the Hamiltonian form of mechanics is preserved. This means that although the variables change from $(q_i,p_i)$ to $(Q_i,P_i)$, the structure of Hamilton’s equations remains the same. The next question is natural: once canonical transformations preserve the form of mechanics, how can we use them to simplify motion, describe very small transformations, and connect symmetries with conserved quantities? That is exactly the road leading to action-angle variables, Lie transformations, and Noether-type symmetry analysis.

## Lie transformations from infinitesimal generators

If one infinitesimal transformation changes $f$ by

$$
\delta f=\varepsilon \{f,G\}
$$

then repeating such infinitesimal steps builds up a finite transformation. This repeated action is called a Lie transformation.

Define the Lie operator $L_G$ by

$$
L_G f=\{f,G\}
$$

Then after a finite transformation generated by $G$, the transformed quantity is written formally as

$$
f'=\exp(\varepsilon L_G)f
$$

Expanding the exponential,

$$
f'=f+\varepsilon L_G f+\frac{\varepsilon^2}{2!}L_G^2 f+\cdots
$$

that is,

$$
f'=f+\varepsilon \{f,G\}+\frac{\varepsilon^2}{2!}\{\{f,G\},G\}+\cdots
$$

So Lie transformations are nothing mysterious. They are just the finite version of infinitesimal canonical transformations built by repeated application of the generator.

This idea is useful because in perturbation theory we often want to remove unwanted terms in the Hamiltonian little by little. Instead of guessing a complicated finite transformation at once, we build it from small generator-driven steps.

## Why the Hamiltonian itself is a generator

Take the infinitesimal generator to be the Hamiltonian itself, $G=H$, and choose the small parameter as $\varepsilon=dt$. Then

$$
\delta q_i=dt\,\{q_i,H\}=dt\,\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}
$$

$$
\delta p_i=dt\,\{p_i,H\}=-dt\,\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}
$$

Hence

$$
\frac{\delta q_i}{dt}=\frac{\partial H}{\partial p_i}, \qquad \frac{\delta p_i}{dt}=-\frac{\partial H}{\partial q_i}
$$

which are Hamilton’s equations. Therefore the Hamiltonian is the generator of time translations in phase space. This connects ordinary dynamics with the general idea of infinitesimal canonical transformations.

## Noether-type symmetry analysis in Hamiltonian form

Now the connection with conservation laws becomes clear. Suppose $G(q_i,p_i,t)$ generates an infinitesimal canonical transformation. If this transformation is a symmetry of the Hamiltonian system, then the Hamiltonian structure remains unchanged under it.

For any function $G$,

$$
\frac{dG}{dt}=\{G,H\}+\frac{\partial G}{\partial t}
$$

So if $G$ has no explicit time dependence and also satisfies

$$
\{G,H\}=0
$$

then

$$
\frac{dG}{dt}=0
$$

Hence $G$ is conserved.

This is the Hamiltonian version of Noether’s idea: a continuous symmetry generator gives a constant of motion.

The logic is now direct:

- a small canonical symmetry is generated by some $G$,
- the change of any quantity is $\delta f=\varepsilon\\{f,G\\}$,
- if the Hamiltonian is invariant under this generator, then $\\{G,H\\}=0$,
- therefore $G$ is conserved.

Examples follow immediately.

If $G=p$, then

$$
\delta q=\varepsilon, \qquad \delta p=0
$$

which is a spatial translation. If the Hamiltonian is unchanged under this translation, then momentum is conserved.

If $G=L_z$, then the transformation is a rotation about the $z$-axis. If the Hamiltonian is rotationally invariant, then $L_z$ is conserved.

So conserved quantities are not merely numbers that happen to stay constant. They are generators of continuous canonical symmetries.

## Action-angle variables from the same point of view

Now move to periodic motion. Suppose the phase-space orbit is closed. Since canonical transformations can simplify motion, we ask whether there exists a new pair of canonical variables adapted to the orbit itself. For periodic systems, the answer is yes: action-angle variables.

For one degree of freedom, define the action variable by

$$
J=\frac{1}{2\pi}\oint p\,dq
$$

where the integral is over one complete closed orbit in phase space. This quantity depends on the orbit, not on the instantaneous point of the particle on that orbit.

The conjugate variable $\theta$ is the angle variable. A canonical transformation can often be found such that the new Hamiltonian depends only on $J$:

$$
H=H(J)
$$

Then Hamilton’s equations become

$$
\dot J=-\frac{\partial H}{\partial \theta}=0
$$

$$
\dot \theta=\frac{\partial H}{\partial J}=\omega(J)
$$

So the motion becomes extremely simple:

- $J$ is constant,
- $\theta$ increases linearly with time.

Thus a complicated oscillatory motion in $(q,p)$ becomes uniform rotation in $(J,\theta)$.

This is again the same philosophy as before. Canonical transformations are used to find variables in which the motion is simplest. In Hamilton–Jacobi theory, the goal was often to make the new variables constant. In action-angle variables, the goal is slightly different: one variable becomes constant and the other evolves uniformly.

For the harmonic oscillator,

$$
H=\frac{p^2}{2m}+\frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 q^2
$$

one finds

$$
J=\frac{E}{\omega}
$$

so

$$
H=\omega J
$$

and hence

$$
\dot \theta=\omega
$$

Therefore

$$
\theta=\omega t+\theta_0
$$

which is exactly the periodic motion of the oscillator expressed in its simplest canonical form.

## Conceptual connection with the previous notes

The flow from the earlier notes to these ideas is now continuous.

From canonical transformations, we learned that mechanics keeps its form if the phase-space one-form changes only by an exact differential. This led to generating functions.

From generating functions, taking a form very close to the identity produced infinitesimal canonical transformations and their generator $G$.

From the infinitesimal generator, the Poisson-bracket relation

$$
\delta f=\varepsilon \{f,G\}
$$

followed.

Repeated infinitesimal transformations gave Lie transformations.

When the generator leaves the Hamiltonian invariant, it becomes a conserved quantity, giving the Hamiltonian form of Noether’s symmetry principle.

Finally, by choosing canonical variables adapted to periodic motion, one reaches action-angle variables.

So the chain is

$$
\text{canonical transformations}
\to
\text{generating functions}
\to
\text{infinitesimal generator } G
\to
\delta f=\varepsilon\{f,G\}
\to
\text{Lie transformation}
\to
\text{symmetry and conservation}
\to
\text{action-angle variables}
$$

## Main points

- Infinitesimal canonical transformations come directly from a generating function close to the identity.
- The generator $G$ gives
  $$
  \delta q_i=\varepsilon \frac{\partial G}{\partial p_i}, \qquad
  \delta p_i=-\varepsilon \frac{\partial G}{\partial q_i}
  $$
- For any phase-space function,
  $$
  \delta f=\varepsilon\{f,G\}
  $$
- Repeated infinitesimal transformations produce Lie transformations.
- The Hamiltonian itself is the generator of time evolution.
- If
  $$
  \{G,H\}+\frac{\partial G}{\partial t}=0
  $$
  then $G$ is conserved.
- Action-angle variables are canonical variables specially adapted to periodic motion.
